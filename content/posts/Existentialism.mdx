# Existentialism and Human Authenticity from Jean-Paul Sartre's View

## Abstract
Existentialism, especially in Jean-Paul Sartre's interpretation, is more than a philosophy. It is a way of life and a way of facing existence. This article explores the foundations of Sartre's existentialism. It examines the central idea of "human authenticity" against "bad faith" (or inauthentic life). Sartre says "existence precedes essence." This means humans are condemned to be free. He emphasizes our absolute responsibility in creating ourselves.

---

## 1. Introduction to Existentialism
Existentialism asks questions about the **meaning of life** in a world that is meaningless. Traditional philosophies believed in a fixed "essence" for humans. But existentialists said that humans first *exist* and then, through their choices, they create their *essence*.

> "Man is nothing other than what he makes of himself." — Jean-Paul Sartre

---

## 2. The Basic Principle: Existence Precedes Essence
Sartre says this is the foundation of his existentialism:
- For objects, **essence comes before existence** (for example, a knife is first designed in the mind, then it is made).
- But for humans, **existence comes before essence**. This means a human is first born. He exists. Then, through his choices, he defines who he is.

This idea means there is no fixed human nature.

---

## 3. Basic Freedom and Being Condemned to Be Free
Sartre says: **"Man is condemned to be free."** He is condemned because he can never escape his freedom. Even "not choosing" is a choice. Freedom in Sartre's philosophy means:
- The ability to say "no" to situations.
- The ability to go beyond the current situation.
- Full responsibility for your actions.

### Absolute Responsibility
Because humans choose their own path, they are not only responsible for their own life. They are also responsible for all of humanity. Every choice creates a model for others.

---

### Bad Faith (Self-Deception)
Sartre says bad faith is trying to deny freedom and responsibility. Examples include:
- Justifying your behavior with social roles (like "I am a waiter, so I have no other choice").
- Believing you have a fixed psychological nature (like "I am naturally shy").
- Blaming outside conditions (like "It is society's fault").

All of these are examples of "inauthentic life." They are ways to escape from your true existence.

---

## 4. The Other and the Look
In his book "Being and Nothingness," Sartre shows that **the look of the Other** is very important in forming the "self." When the Other looks at me, he defines me as an *object* in his world. This look can:
- Take away my freedom and limit my existence.
- Push me toward authenticity. Through the Other's look, I become aware of my freedom and responsibility.

### Conflict or Coexistence?
For Sartre, my relationship with the Other is always full of conflict. Everyone tries to impose their freedom on the freedom of others. However, authentic existentialism uses this conflict to create new meaning.

---

## 5. Existentialism as Humanism
In his famous essay **"Existentialism and Human Authenticity"** (sometimes translated as "Existentialism is a Humanism"), Sartre says this philosophy is not pessimistic. It gives dignity to humans because:
- It sees **humans as the only source of values**.
- It calls humans to create meaning constantly.
- It avoids hopeless nihilism and invites passionate action.

> Sartre's humanism means: "Man is nothing other than his own project. Only through action does he give meaning to existence."

---

## 6. Conclusion: Living with Authenticity
Sartre's existentialism is a perfect mirror of modern human anxiety, freedom, and responsibility. Authentic living means living in **constant awareness of your choices** and **accepting their consequences**. An authentic person does not try to fit into a pre-made mold. Instead, he recreates himself at every moment. In this way, he reaches his true existence.

Finally, Sartre's message can be summarized in one sentence:

> **"Man is forced to create himself. And this creation is the only path to authenticity."**
